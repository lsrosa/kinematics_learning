\section{Results}\label{sec: results}

\subsection{Testing Environment}\label{subsec: test env}

We build upon the \texttt{Reacher-v4} environment from OpenAI \gym \cite{gym}, which consists of a 2D serial manipulator with $2$ joints simulated using \mujoco \cite{todorov2012mujoco}.
%
In order to evaluate the proposed approach for more realistic robots, we extend the environment enabling for 3D, and adding models for robots up to $7$ joints\footnote{Publicly available source code: \href{https://github.com/lsrosa/kinematics\_learning}{https://github.com/lsrosa/kinematics\_learning}}.

The $\nn$ modules are implemented using \torch \cite{pytorch}, allowing for integration with \gym, and the derivatives used in Section \ref{subsec: loss and acc} are computed using \torch's \autograd \cite{paszke2017automatic}.

For the sake of briefness we restrict the results presented in this section for the 3D case, as robots with more than $3$ joints usually act in the three dimensional space. 

\subsection{Parameters Tuning}\label{subsec: tuning}

In order to assure appropriate hyper-parameters for $\nn$, we use \ray \cite{liaw2018tune} with \optuna \cite{optuna2019} as search engine and a \softwareFont{Asynchronous Hyper-Band} \cite{li2018massively} as scheduler.
%
The hyper-parameters are sampled in the interval presented in Table \ref{tab: hyperparams search space}, 
%
We empirically found that $\nSamples=100$ and $\nIterations=25$ are appropriated values tuning hyper-parameters for Algorithm \ref{alg: train nn}.

		\bottomrule

During the tuning process, the average loss over $10$ learning trials $\lossTune$, $\epochs$, and the total number of parameters in $\nn$ ($\nParams$) are passed to \optuna every $50$ $\epochs$ until $\lossTune<=0.005$ or $\epochs=2000$ are reached.
%
Optimizing $\lossTune$ leads for hyper-parameters which allow for effective learning the forward kinematics; optimizing $\epochs$ incentivize the optimizer finding hyper-parameters that improve the learning sampling-efficiency; and optimizing $\nParams$ gives priority for smaller networks.

A total of $50$ hyper-parameters samples are computed and collected by \ray, and the final values are given by the sample with smallest $\lossTune$ among the Pareto dominating samples according to $\lossTune$, $\epochs$, and $\nParams$. 
%
Table \ref{tab: tune fkine} presents the hyper-parameters found for $\nnMono$ and $\nnLink$ for robots with $\nJoints=\{2,\ldots, 7\}$. 
%
The found hyper-parameters values demonstrate the consequences of using a monolithic architecture: the complexity of the function being approximated grows with $\nJoints$ (as described in Equation \ref{eq: x estimate in function of q}), and hence a larger \gls{nn} is more suitable.
%
In comparison, the function being approximated with the proposed architecture is constant in $\nJoints$, and increasing $\nJoints$ increases the number of approximators linearly.
%
As result, the proposed architecture is expected to scale better with $\nJoints$ than its monolithic counterpart, both in precision and sample efficiency.

\subsection{Training, Loss, and Accuracy Results}\label{subsec: loss and acc}

The models are trained with the hyper-parameters found according to Section \ref{subsec: tuning}.
%
The training budget is set as $2000$ epochs with $\nSamples=100$ and $\nIterations=25$.

Figure \ref{fig: loss fkines} presents the losses when learning $\nnMono$ and $\nnLink$, which shows that the proposed architecture outperforms the monolithic approach found used in the literature.

\begin{figure*}
	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fkine_link_x_mono}
	\caption{Loss comparison between $\nnMono$ and $\nnLink$. The solid line and shaded area represent the mean loss and the area between minimum and maximum losses over $30$ trials.}
	\label{fig: loss fkines}
\end{figure*}

For qualitative comparison, Figure \ref{fig: joints position} presents the joints position estimation obtained by $\nnMono$ and $\nnLink$ for $\nJoints=7$ only (other joint numbers cases are omitted for briefness), in which the higher accuracy can be seem.

\begin{figure*}
	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth, height=2in]{fkine_predictions_x}
	\caption{Joints position estimation comparison for $\nnMono$ and $\nnLink$ for $100$ steps.}
	\label{fig: joints position}
\end{figure*}

Figure \ref{fig: errors} presents the joint prediction error for $1000$ samples for the robot with $\nJoints=7$, showing that the $\nnLink$ achieves a higher accuracy than $\nnMono$, which is expected due to the improved learning efficiency shown in Figure \ref{fig: loss fkines}.
%
\begin{figure*}
	\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fkine_errors_hist_x}
	\caption{Accuracy comparison for $\nnMono$ and $\nnLink$.}
	\label{fig: errors}
\end{figure*}


We can observe a characteristic for the models accuracies: the error increases for the further joints. 
%
For $\nnLink$, this results from the error propagation from one joint to another since estimation from  the previous joint is used for computing the following joint's estimation; for $\nnMono$ the increasing error is lead by the fact that the function being approximated for the latter joints is more complex (see Equation \ref{eq: x estimate in function of q}).
%
It is important to emphasize that using a monolithic architecture for estimating the forward kinematics is inappropriate, as the \gls{nn} outputs are related to functions with varying complexity, leading non-uniform learning in the network, which is prone to fall into local-minima.
%
In fact, we speculate that the results presented in Figure \ref{fig: loss fkines} for $\nnMono$ are explained by such local-minima: once the loss drops due to sufficient accuracy on learning the first joints, the optimization process struggles to refine the parameters, leading to the very slowly decaying loss observed after an initial sudden drop. 

Next, to demonstrate the accuracy impacts, we estimate the end-effector velocity $\dot{\pos}_j = \left[\jacob\dot{\rotAngle{}}\right]_j$, where $\jacob$ is the unknown Jacobian matrix of the robot, as $\prediction{\dot{\pos}}_j = \left[\frac{\partial\nn}{\partial\rotAngle{}}\dot{\rotAngle{}}\right]_j$.
%
That is, we use the learned network for estimating the unknown Jacobian of the model, which has its well-known applications in inverse kinematics \cite{waldron1985study} computation, Cartesian impedance controllers \cite{sanchez2010cartesian}, and tracking controllers \cite{chen2017tracking}.
%
Figure \ref{fig: ee vel} shows the velocity estimation and ground-truth obtained from the simulator for $\nDims=3$ and $\nJoints=7$.
%
The better accuracy of the proposed architecture indicates its adequacy for being used in higher level applications, while the monolithic architecture poor accuracy might impact higher level applications badly.
